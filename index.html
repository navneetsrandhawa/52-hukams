The Background

The 52 Hukams emerge from a decisive and sacred moment in Sikh history, forming a bridge between the Gurus and the self-governing Khalsa.

When Guru Gobind Singh Sahib, the tenth manifestation of the Timeless Being personified as Guru Nanak Dev Ji, reached Nanded (Deccan), a fatal attack was made on his life by two Afghans sent by the Nawab of Sirhind. Though he initially recovered under the surgical treatment of a British doctor, the wound later reopened when he drew the string of a bow.

As the end drew near, the True Guru declined further surgical treatment. He adorned his sacred body with arms, mounted his horse, and prepared for his soul’s union with the Supreme Being. In these final moments, Guru Gobind Singh Sahib, the embodiment of indomitable spirit, delivered essential instructions to the Gursikhs present.

These teachings were recorded by Baba Ram Koer Ji, the great-grandson of Baba Buddha Ji. They were later published widely by Sikh bodies and read with eagerness and devotion by the Sikh community.

Guru Gobind Singh Sahib was the embodiment of Guru Nanak Dev Ji’s light, preaching the same ideology and teachings at the cost of his life. Terms such as Fateh, Singh, and Sardar, bestowed upon the Khalsa by Guru Gobind Singh Ji, are deeply rooted in Guru Nanak Dev Ji’s bani. The principles of uncut hair, the sword, and moral discipline (Sil, Jat, Kachh) are clearly expressed in Gurbani enshrined within the Guru Granth Sahib.
